History of Nigeria's Ministry of Foreign Affairs

Nigeria's Ministry of Foreign Affairs traces its roots to the country's independence on 1 October 1960, when the conduct of external relations passed into Nigerian hands for the first time. Over more than six decades, it has guided the nation's evolution from a newly independent state into one of Africa's foremost diplomatic powers.

1960

Independence & Founding

At independence on 1 October 1960, Nigeria established the Ministry of External Affairs (as it was then known) under the leadership of Jaja Wachukwu, Nigeria's first Minister of External Affairs. The Ministry immediately set about building diplomatic relations with countries across the world and articulating Nigeria's non-aligned foreign policy stance during the Cold War.

1961–1965

Early Diplomacy & UN Engagement

Nigeria joined the United Nations in 1960 and quickly made its voice heard on issues of African liberation and decolonisation. Under Jaja Wachukwu, Nigeria championed the cause of African unity, was an early advocate within the Organisation of African Unity (OAU) founded in 1963, and sought to position Nigeria as a bridge between the developed and developing world.

1967–1970

Civil War & Diplomatic Resilience

During the Nigerian Civil War (1967–1970), the Ministry played a critical role in managing Nigeria's international image, securing diplomatic support for the Federal Government and countering international recognition of Biafra. Despite the enormous pressure of the conflict, Nigeria's diplomatic corps maintained its global engagements and helped secure a post-war reintegration that was widely admired internationally.

1975–1979

Oil Boom & Assertive Diplomacy

Riding the wave of oil wealth and economic confidence, Nigeria in the mid-1970s pursued a more assertive, Africa-centred foreign policy. The Ministry coordinated Nigeria's decisive role in the liberation of southern African states, including financial and material support for liberation movements in Angola, Mozambique, Zimbabwe and South Africa. This period established Nigeria's credentials as a continental power committed to African self-determination.

1986–1993

Bold Policy Vision Under Akinyemi & ECOWAS Leadership

Minister Bolaji Akinyemi articulated the "Concert of Medium Powers" doctrine, seeking to place Nigeria at the head of a coalition of influential mid-tier states shaping global affairs. Nigeria's role in establishing ECOMOG — the ECOWAS Monitoring Group — in 1990 to intervene in the Liberian civil war demonstrated the Ministry's capacity to translate diplomatic vision into concrete peacekeeping action.

1999–2007

Democratic Renewal & Global Re-engagement

The return to democracy in 1999 under President Obasanjo enabled Nigeria to re-engage with the international community as a responsible, democratic partner. The Ministry led Nigeria's participation in establishing the African Union (2002), driving the New Partnership for Africa's Development (NEPAD) and securing Nigeria's role as a founding pillar of the AU's Peace and Security Council. Nigeria also deepened trade and investment ties globally during this period.

2015–2023

The Onyeama Era & COVID-19 Diplomacy

Under Minister Geoffrey Onyeama, Nigeria deepened its multilateral engagements, worked to reform the Ministry's internal structures, and navigated the complex global landscape of the COVID-19 pandemic — coordinating the evacuation of thousands of stranded Nigerians abroad and managing Nigeria's bilateral health cooperation. The Ministry also continued to advocate for African representation in global governance institutions.

2023 – Present

The 4D Doctrine & Nigeria's Renewed Global Ambition

Under the Tinubu administration and Minister Yusuf Maitama Tuggar, the Ministry launched the bold 4D Foreign Policy Framework — centred on Democracy, Development, Demography and Diaspora. This framework seeks to harness Nigeria's youthful population, diaspora networks and democratic credentials to drive economic growth, attract investment and amplify Nigeria's voice as Africa's leading nation on the global stage.

List of All Nigerian Foreign Ministers

Nigeria has been served by a distinguished line of Foreign Ministers since independence. Each brought a unique perspective and contributed to shaping the nation's place in the world.

Note: The Ministry was originally called the Ministry of External Affairs; it was renamed the Ministry of Foreign Affairs in 1975.

# Name Tenure Administration Notes
1 Jaja Wachukwu 1961 – 1965 Balewa (First Republic) First Minister of External Affairs; strong voice for African liberation at the UN
2 Nuhu Bamali 1971 – 1975 Gowon Managed Nigeria's post-civil war diplomacy and global re-integration
3 Brigadier Joe Garba 1975 – 1978 Murtala / Obasanjo (military) Spearheaded Nigeria's support for liberation movements in southern Africa
4 Maj.-Gen. Henry Adefope 1979 – 1983 Shagari (Second Republic) Managed Nigeria's oil-boom-era diplomatic activism
5 Prof. Ibrahim Gambari 1984 – 1985 Buhari (military) Later served as UN Under-Secretary-General and African Union–UN Joint Chief Mediator
6 Prof. Bolaji Akinyemi 1985 – 1987 Babangida Originator of the "Concert of Medium Powers" doctrine and the Technical Aid Corps
7 Maj.-Gen. Ike Nwachukwu 1987 – 1990 Babangida Coordinated Nigeria's role in establishing ECOMOG (ECOWAS Monitoring Group)
8 Rilwanu Lukman 1990; 1993 Babangida / Shonekan Served two short terms; also former OPEC Secretary-General
9 Mahmud Aliyu Tukur 1990 – 1992 Babangida
10 Baba Gana Kingibe 1993 – 1995 Abacha Presidential running-mate of MKO Abiola in the annulled 1993 elections
11 Tom Ikimi 1995 – 1999 Abacha / Abubakar Managed Nigeria's diplomacy during the difficult Abacha isolation period
12 Sule Lamido 1999 – 2002 Obasanjo Led Nigeria's post-military democratic re-engagement with the international community
13 Amb. Oluyemi Adeniji 2003 – 2006 Obasanjo Former UN Special Representative; helped establish the African Union
14 Amb. Joy Ogwu 2006 – 2010 Obasanjo / Yar'Adua First female Foreign Minister of Nigeria; former UN Security Council President
15 Odein Ajumogobia (SAN) 2010 – 2011 Jonathan
16 Amb. Olugbenga Ashiru 2011 – 2015 Jonathan Career diplomat; managed Nigeria's engagement during the Boko Haram security crisis
17 Geoffrey Onyeama 2015 – 2023 Buhari Longest-serving recent Foreign Minister; guided Nigeria's COVID-19 diaspora diplomacy
18 Amb. Yusuf Maitama Tuggar Current 2023 – Present Tinubu Architect of the 4D Foreign Policy Framework; former Nigerian Ambassador to Germany
